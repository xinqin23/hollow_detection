\title{Shape vs.\ Appearance: Comparing DINOv2 and YOLOv8 on Original and Hollow Contour Images}

\begin{abstract}
We investigate how well vision models can classify images using only shape outlines (Canny edge contours) compared to full-color originals. We compare two architectures---DINOv2 (a self-supervised Vision Transformer) and YOLOv8n-cls (a lightweight CNN classifier)---on two datasets: CIFAR-10 (10-class object recognition) and an Overwatch character recognition dataset (14-class game screenshots). On CIFAR-10, both models achieve $\sim$96\% on originals, but diverge on contours: YOLOv8n retains 70.4\% while DINOv2 drops to 51.6\%. On Overwatch, YOLOv8n reaches 65.8\% on originals and 38.1\% on contours, while DINOv2 achieves 56.5\% and 13.4\% respectively. Across both datasets, fine-tuned CNNs consistently leverage shape information more effectively than frozen self-supervised features.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

A long-standing question in visual recognition is the relative importance of shape versus texture in how neural networks classify objects~\cite{geirhos2019imagenet}. Human perception is strongly shape-biased, yet convolutional neural networks have been shown to rely more on local texture cues. Self-supervised Vision Transformers such as DINO~\cite{caron2021emerging} have been reported to develop more shape-aware representations, attending to object boundaries even without explicit supervision.

In this study, we test this claim empirically by removing all texture and color information from images, leaving only Canny edge contours (hollow outlines). We evaluate on two datasets of different nature:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{CIFAR-10}: A standard 10-class object recognition benchmark (50k train, 10k test; native $32\times32$).
    \item \textbf{Overwatch Frames}: A custom 14-class dataset of game character screenshots (1,309 train, 336 test; native $3420\times2138$), containing both normal and occluded views.
\end{itemize}

We compare two contrasting architectures:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{DINOv2} (ViT-S/14): A frozen self-supervised Vision Transformer with a linear classification head trained on the target data.
    \item \textbf{YOLOv8n-cls}: A lightweight convolutional classifier, fully fine-tuned from pretrained ImageNet weights.
\end{itemize}

\section{Method}

\subsection{Dataset Preparation}

For both datasets, we generate contour versions by: (1) converting to grayscale, (2) applying Canny edge detection (thresholds: 100, 200), and (3) saving the binary edge map as a 3-channel PNG. All images are resized to $224 \times 224$ at training/inference time.

For the Overwatch dataset, images from both the \texttt{normal/} and \texttt{occluded/} subfolders are merged per character, then split 80/20 into train/test sets.

\subsection{DINOv2 Setup}

We use the \texttt{dinov2\_vits14} model from the official DINOv2 release. The ViT-S/14 backbone is frozen, and we train only a linear classifier (384-dim $\rightarrow$ $N$ classes) on top of the \texttt{[CLS]} token output. Training runs for 100 epochs with the Adam optimizer (lr = $1\times10^{-3}$) and cosine annealing. Batch size is 256 for CIFAR-10 and 64 for Overwatch. Data augmentation (random horizontal flip, color jitter) is applied for Overwatch.

\subsection{YOLOv8n-cls Setup}

We use the Ultralytics YOLOv8n-cls model, pretrained on ImageNet. All layers are fine-tuned for 30 epochs with the AdamW optimizer (auto-tuned learning rate), image size $224 \times 224$. Batch size is 256 for CIFAR-10 and 64 for Overwatch.

\subsection{Hardware}

All experiments were run on a single NVIDIA RTX 6000 Ada Generation (48 GB) with PyTorch 2.6 and CUDA 12.4.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Cross-Dataset Consistency}

The relative ranking of models is consistent across both datasets: YOLOv8n (fine-tuned) outperforms DINOv2 (frozen) on both original and contour images, with the gap widening dramatically on contours. Table~\ref{tab:summary} summarizes the accuracy drops.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Accuracy drop (\%) from original to contour.}
    \label{tab:summary}
    \begin{tabular}{lcc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Model} & \textbf{CIFAR-10 drop} & \textbf{Overwatch drop} \\
        \midrule
        DINOv2 & $-44.3$ & $-43.2$ \\
        YOLOv8n-cls & $-25.6$ & $-27.7$ \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Remarkably, both models lose roughly the same \emph{proportion} of accuracy when switching to contours, regardless of dataset. DINOv2 loses $\sim$44\% (absolute) across both; YOLOv8n loses $\sim$26--28\%.

\subsection{Why Does YOLO Outperform DINO on Contours?}

The key difference lies in the training paradigm:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{DINOv2 (frozen backbone + linear probe)}: The features were learned via self-supervised pretraining on natural images rich in texture and color. When these cues are removed, the frozen representations may not encode the remaining shape information in a linearly separable way. The model cannot adapt its internal representations to the contour domain.

    \item \textbf{YOLOv8n (full fine-tuning)}: All layers are updated during training, allowing the network to adapt its low-level and mid-level features specifically for edge/contour patterns. The convolutional inductive bias (local receptive fields, translation equivariance) may also be better suited to processing sparse edge maps.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Implications}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Frozen SSL features are domain-sensitive}: DINOv2's general-purpose representations degrade substantially when the input distribution shifts from natural images to binary edge maps. This holds across both a standard benchmark (CIFAR-10) and a domain-specific dataset (Overwatch).

    \item \textbf{Shape alone is partially sufficient}: YOLOv8n achieves 70\% on CIFAR-10 contours and 38\% on Overwatch contours (vs.\ 7.1\% random), demonstrating that Canny edges retain meaningful classification signal.

    \item \textbf{Adaptation matters more than architecture}: The consistent advantage of YOLO over DINO on contours is primarily due to fine-tuning vs.\ frozen features, not CNN vs.\ ViT architecture.

    \item \textbf{Small datasets amplify the gap}: On Overwatch ($\sim$93 images/class), the limited data makes it even harder for a frozen linear probe to find a good decision boundary, especially in the unfamiliar contour domain.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

We compared DINOv2 and YOLOv8n-cls on classifying images from two datasets---CIFAR-10 and Overwatch character screenshots---in both original form and as Canny edge contours. Across both datasets, YOLOv8n (fine-tuned) consistently outperforms DINOv2 (frozen) on contours by $\sim$19--25 percentage points, while the two models perform comparably or with a smaller gap on originals. The primary factor is training strategy (fine-tuning vs.\ frozen backbone), not architecture. Future work could explore fine-tuning DINOv2's backbone, using adapter layers, or testing with larger training sets.
